# KNIME → RapidMiner Migration — The Ownership Pivot

> A Saturday morning devil's-advocate conversation that reframed the entire KNIME phase-out fight from "defend the platform" to "own the migration." Captured verbatim at Phil's request — do not edit this file, it's meant as a reference record of the strategic reframe while it was still hot.

**Context going in:** Siemens acquired Altair (RapidMiner). Board decided "we use what we sell." Current lean from CIO board + sounding board: migrate from KNIME to RapidMiner over ~18 months, freeze new KNIME onboarding now. Phil leads the cross-company **Low-Code Circle**, founded in response to this threat. No formal mandate — only the ability to raise business concerns. First senior call happened Friday (Apr 10). ITDA call coming next. Phil's stated position going into the conversation: *"peaceful coexistence until feature parity,"* with *"keep KNIME as is"* at the core of his heart.

## Claude — Devil's Advocate

Good. Energy is a 9, I'll push hard. Five angles, then the uncomfortable question, then the Monday test.

---

### 1. "Peaceful coexistence" is a technical answer to an identity problem

The board didn't decide *"we'll evaluate RapidMiner on the merits."* They decided *"we use what we sell."* That's an **identity** decision, not a technical one. Feature parity, business continuity, integration depth — all of those are arguments that only work if the decision is technical. You're bringing a feature-comparison knife to an identity fight.

The current lean (migrate over 1.5 years, freeze new onboarding) is **already** a concession against "use what we sell." You're asking for a concession on top of a concession. Every month of coexistence is a month your side looks like it's losing slowly.

### 2. "Until feature parity is achieved" — who holds that clock?

Not you. Not your team. **RapidMiner's product team holds it.** Their mandate is to deliver parity, and eventually they'll declare it — whether it's true or not. The CIO board, having already decided "use what we sell," will take "RapidMiner says parity is here" as sufficient cover.

You've built your counter-proposal on a gate that the *other side* controls. That's not a defensive position — that's a countdown.

### 3. "Business continuity" is the wrong frame for finance

It's not wrong on the facts — finance integrations *are* your strongest concrete argument. It's wrong on the *language*. "Business continuity" is so worn out that executive brains skip past it automatically. It's dismissed before it's heard.

What would land instead: **named workflows, named euros, named compliance exposures.** Not *"finance depends on this"* but *"the monthly closing workflow for SFS touches 14 KNIME components, owned by [name], runs against SAP ECC, and a migration failure blocks the Day-5 close. Estimated exposure: €X, risk window: Q3."* Abstractions lose. Named consequences land because they make a specific human accountable for denying them.

You probably have this data. Are you putting it in front of the ITDA call?

### 4. You're not in the room. Community pressure doesn't move board decisions.

This is the biggest one and I'm going to be direct about it:

The Low-Code Circle is a cross-company community initiative with **no mandate**, whose job is to *"raise concerns from a business perspective."* That is, by design, the exact level of power the CIO and sounding boards are built to absorb and move past. Boards don't reverse course because a community raised concerns. They reverse course when:

- A business unit owner says *"this will miss my Q3 number"* in their own voice
- Legal or audit says *"this creates a finding"*
- A customer — internal or external — escalates to a level above the board

You are bringing community-level pressure to a board-level decision. The asymmetry is enormous, and if you don't acknowledge it, you'll spend 18 months doing work that was never going to bend the outcome.

### 5. "Keep KNIME as is" is probably unwinnable — but "define the rules of the migration" is not

You rejected the third option — *lean in as premium migration partner, give up on KNIME, lose credibility.* I want to push back on that rejection, because I think you dismissed it in the wrong form.

**The form that works:** not "full steam ahead, give up on KNIME," but **"we write the migration playbook."** You set the technical criteria for what 'feature parity' actually means — framed around finance safety. You gate the decisions. You own what gets migrated first, in what order, with what fallbacks.

The person who **defines the rules of a migration controls it.** The person who tries to stop it from outside the room gets run over by it.

That's not giving up on KNIME. That's the only position from which KNIME actually gets protected — because you're the one drawing the lines the migration can't cross.

---

### The uncomfortable question

You said: *"at the core of my heart, it's keep KNIME as is."*

Is that a strategic position, or is that a loyalty reflex? You're "Mr. KNIME" inside Siemens. The fight to keep KNIME is also, underneath, a fight to keep that identity intact. I'm not saying that's wrong — I'm saying it's worth separating from the strategic question so you can see both clearly.

If KNIME is replaced by RapidMiner over 18 months, but **you** become *"the person who ran the most successful data-science migration in Siemens history, protected finance closing from disruption, and built the Low-Code Circle into the thing every BU consults before any platform decision"* — is that actually a bad outcome for Phil?

Yesterday's negotiation training idea told me something: **part of you already knows the identity needs to be more portable than any one platform.** The fallback instinct is right. The question is whether you bring that same portability logic to the KNIME fight itself.

If even one of these landed, here's what changes Monday:

It's me saying the *position* you're arguing from is structurally weaker than you feel it is right now — and the feeling of being "leader of the pack" on Friday might be the exact thing masking it. Which angle stings the most? That's usually the one worth sitting with over the weekend.

## Claude — the credibility answer (the strategic hinge of the whole pivot)

This is the question that decides whether the pivot lands or blows up. It needs to be handled with precision, not cleverness.

### The reframe that unlocks it

The Low-Code Circle's **stated purpose** was *"raise concerns from a business perspective."*

Its **underlying purpose** was *"protect the business from platform decisions made without business context."*

"Raising concerns" was one *tactic* in service of that purpose. "Owning the migration" is a **different tactic in service of the exact same purpose.** The pivot doesn't abandon what I promised the community. It *upgrades the tactic* once I had enough information to see that defense was going to lose.

That's not rationalization. That's literally what happened this weekend — I learned something new (the board made an identity decision, not a technical one; the feature parity clock is held by the other side; community pressure doesn't move boards), and I updated my strategy in response. **That's not betrayal. That's leadership.** The alternative — keeping a losing strategy to protect my image — is the *actual* betrayal of the community's trust, because it would leave them fighting a war they can't win.

The narrative bridge in one sentence:

> *"We founded the Circle because we were afraid the business would be ignored. We raised concerns. We got heard — and what we learned inside those rooms changed what we think the right move is. The concerns were real. The answer is bigger than we thought."*

### Four moves that protect credibility — and one that destroys it

**1. Name the shift explicitly.** Do not pretend this was always the plan. The pivot IS real. Transparency about the evolution is what makes it land. People can smell retroactive rationalization instantly — and that's exactly what critics are waiting to accuse me of. So beat them to it. Say: *"Three weeks ago I thought X. Here's what I learned. Here's why I now think Y — and here's why Y actually honors the concerns we raised, better than X ever could."*

**2. Embed the original concerns as non-negotiables in the new plan.** Every concern the Circle raised — finance integration depth, business continuity, compliance exposure, user pain — becomes a **gate** the migration must pass through. The Circle's work isn't wasted. The Circle *defined the rules* that the migration has to follow. That's more power than raising concerns ever had.

**3. Give the community a job, not a eulogy.** The Circle doesn't become obsolete under the new plan — it becomes the **quality control layer**. Every workflow that gets migrated requires sign-off from the owner's BU representative in the Circle. Each member keeps power over their own domain, and in many cases *gains* power because now they're gatekeepers, not petitioners. Frame it in the announcement: *"Your role in the Circle just got more important, not less."*

**4. Name the career dimension honestly — to my inner circle first, before anyone else has to ask.** Counterintuitive but critical: **trying to hide that this is also good for my career will damage credibility more than naming it.** Everyone will figure it out anyway. The question is whether I look like I was sneaking it past them or whether I looked it in the eye. Go to the 3-5 people in the Circle whose trust matters most, privately, before anything public, and say:

> *"I'm not pretending this isn't also good for me. It is. Being the guy who runs this migration could be the biggest moment of my Siemens career, and I'm not going to lie about that part. But I also genuinely believe it's the right move for the business and the community — and I want you in the room holding me accountable to making sure it actually is. If at any point you think I'm drifting toward career over community, I want you to say it to my face. That's the deal."*

That's disarming because it *names the fear the critic would have used against me* and then *invites them to stay and hold me accountable.* It converts potential attackers into co-owners of the outcome.

**And the one that destroys credibility:** trying to spin the pivot as *consistent* with the prior position. *"Actually, if you read what I said carefully, I was always open to..."* — that's the move that burns trust irrecoverably. Never do that. Always acknowledge the shift.

### Accept losses upfront

Not everyone will come with me. Some Circle members joined because they love KNIME and wanted a defender. Those people will read the pivot as abandonment no matter how well it's framed. **Decide now** who I *must* keep (finance BU leaders, the sponsors who can unlock migration budget, the 3-5 quietly competent people who do the actual work) versus who I *can afford to lose* (KNIME purists, theatrical skeptics, people who were there to complain rather than build).

Trying to carry everyone is how pivots die. Pick the keepers, protect those relationships hard, and let the rest land where they land.

### The strategic reframe for the community itself

The Circle was founded as a **defensive** structure. *"Protect us from the bad decision."* This pivot converts it into an **offensive** structure. *"Define how the decision gets executed."* Same people, same mandate, different posture. And here's the uncomfortable truth I can say out loud: **defense was losing.** Every honest member of the Circle already feels that. The pivot gives them a way out of a losing war into a winning one — without asking them to admit they were wrong to be defensive in the first place. I owe them that escape route. Offer it generously.

*Subject: Before I take this wider — I need you to push back on me*

Three weeks ago I walked into the first senior call ready to fight for KNIME.

I had the slides. I had the arguments. I had a team of people who believed the same thing I did — that the business was about to get steamrolled by a decision made in rooms where the business wasn't sitting.

I still believe that second part.

What changed is what I think we should do about it.

Let me tell you what I saw in those rooms.

The decision to move to RapidMiner isn't a technical decision. It was never going to be one. *"We use what we sell"* isn't a feature comparison — it's an identity statement, and you don't win identity fights with feature lists. I brought a knife to the wrong kind of fight.

I also saw something else. The people pushing the migration aren't villains. They have a mandate that doesn't leave them much room, and they *know* — they know — that finance is going to get hurt when this lands. They just don't have the muscle memory for the business side that we have. They're not ignoring us. They're hoping someone like us shows up.

That's where it clicked.

Here's what I now think we should do. Not fight the migration. **Own it.**

Not *"full steam ahead, forget KNIME."* Not *"roll over."* Something very different: we walk into the next ITDA conversation and offer to run the migration ourselves. GBS Low-Code Service, not IT. We take the risk. We take the timeline. We take the blame if it breaks. And in return, we take the thing we've always wanted — the seat at the table where decisions about our users actually get made.

Because here's the part that made me sit up straight this weekend: remember Qlik and Tableau to Power BI?

Everyone remembers.

Everyone has a scar.

That migration happened *to* the business, not *with* the business, and we're still paying for it in finance today. This one doesn't have to go that way. We can be the reason it doesn't. Not out of charity — out of the fact that we're the only group inside Siemens who actually lives inside those finance workflows. IT offloads the risk they don't know how to carry. We pick up the thing we were built to do. Nobody loses.

And yeah — I need to say this part out loud, because you'd see through me if I didn't.

This is also good for me.

Being the person who runs the most successful data platform migration Siemens has ever seen is the kind of thing that changes a career. I'm 51. I have maybe 5-7 years left where a promotion like that is still on the table. I'm not going to pretend I don't see that window. I do. And I want it.

But I want it on terms I can live with. Which is why I'm writing to *you* first — before the Circle, before the ITDA call, before anything goes public. I need the people whose judgment I actually trust to look me in the eye and tell me if this is the move, or if I'm kidding myself.

And if it is the move, I need you to stay. Not to nod along. To stay *in the room* and hold me accountable to the thing we started the Circle for in the first place — protecting the business from a migration run by people who don't know the workflows.

That part doesn't change. The concerns we raised become the gates the migration has to pass through. Every workflow. Every finance closing. Every signed-off integration. If at any point you think I'm drifting toward "career over community," I want you to say it to my face. That's the deal.

The Circle was built to defend. I'm asking us to attack instead.

Same fight. Better ground.

Tell me what you think. Honestly. I'd rather hear it from you now than find out later I was fooling myself.

Phil "Changing My Mind in Public" Kowalski
