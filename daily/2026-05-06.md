## Toby's Visit — Role Reversal

Toby arrived around 5pm on short notice — we agreed Monday and made it work. Showed him some KNIME things he had questions about, and he confirmed today it was productive and helpful.

The interesting part was the philosophical layer underneath. Toby was always the intrapreneur, I was more the standard worker. He left his original org to try something new — and right now he's not satisfied. Meanwhile, I'm thriving. Complete role reversal.

That hit me as a reminder: my current good situation isn't a given. I don't just deserve this by default. You have to actively work on building and maintaining an environment that's positive, constructive, motivating, inspiring. Seeing Toby's reality up close made that concrete — not as a warning, but as gratitude paired with responsibility.

## Paula and Snow

Paula came back from horse riding in tears. I could see it through the window before she reached the door. Snow — her most beloved horse — had died. She found out the worst possible way: went to his box after the lesson to give him his apple, found it empty, and another girl just said, "Didn't you know? He's dead."

She cried the whole evening.

What makes death hit so hard is its finality. It's non-reversible. From this point on, you live without that being, and you don't yet know how. I know this from losing both my parents early. I can relate to her on that level.

As her dad, I just held her. Gave her a shoulder to cry on. Didn't try to fix it, didn't lecture.

There's a second side to this that I believe deeply: if life were endless, it would be far less valuable. The possibility of loss is what makes relationships — with a horse, a person, anything — precious. I didn't tell her that. She's twelve and she was sad. That wisdom is for later, when she's ready. For now, the shoulder was enough.
