# Sunday, April 5, 2026 — Easter Sunday

## What Happened

**The big day.** Got up early to be in time for church. Had breakfast, then one more rehearsal before the 10 a.m. mass. The rehearsal was okay-ish — not our sharpest, but enough. And then we performed. Overall, it went well. Really well.

**The Hallelujah — one slip.** During the Hallelujah, I came in a bit too fast at one point. My fellow bass singer gave me a nod — the kind that says "slow down" without a word. I caught it, adjusted. But it stuck with me. The rest of the day, that little voice: "Did everyone hear it? Did I ruin weeks of practice for the whole choir?" I'm leaning towards: no, I didn't. I gave the best I had, and at that exact moment, that *was* my best. Not because I was lazy, not because I wasn't watching the Dirigent. I was watching. It just happened. Mistakes happen. I need to accept that — and I mostly do, but the audio recording landed today, and I'm a bit afraid to listen. Afraid I'll hear it too clearly and feel ashamed. I'll get to it. Not today.

**The Benedictus — again.** Just like in Wednesday's rehearsal, the Benedictus hit me. Tears in my eyes, though probably nobody noticed. It starts so quiet, just voices, and builds into something that goes straight through you. If the Hallelujah was the moment of imperfection, the Benedictus was the moment of grace. That's a fair trade.

**Lunch with the community.** After the mass, the majority of the choir went to lunch together — our priest, the violinists who joined for the mass, good conversation. The kind of meal where you're still wearing the afterglow of what you just did together.

**Quiet afternoon and evening.** Computer in the afternoon, some creative work. TV with the family in the evening, another episode of Yellowstone. Nothing dramatic, nothing heavy. The body and mind just settling after a big morning.

**Thirty years without my father.** Easter Sunday 2026 — exactly thirty years since my father died. He was 50. I'm 51 now. One year older than he ever got to be. That realization lands differently every time it surfaces, and today it surfaced during mass. I didn't mention it to the family. Today is Paula's twelfth birthday — April 6th — and I didn't want to cast a shadow on her celebration. So I carried it quietly.

I've said it in a prior entry: there are wounds on your heart that never fully heal, and that's okay, because they make you who you are. After thirty years, it's not a sharp pain anymore. It's a soft sadness — the kind you recognize like an old acquaintance. Die Zeit heilt alle Wunden. Time heals all wounds. But healing doesn't mean forgetting. It means the sting becomes a warmth, eventually. And looking back at who I've become in these thirty years — a husband, a father, someone who finished an Ironman and sings in a choir and leads teams and builds things — I think he'd be proud. I hope so.

- **Mood:** Proud (the performance happened, we did it), vulnerable (the slip, the audio fear), reflective (thirty years), quietly grieving (parents, softly)

## Key Takeaway

A day where you sing the Hallelujah imperfectly, cry during the Benedictus, carry a thirty-year grief through a celebration, and protect your daughter's birthday from your sadness — that's not a contradiction. That's just what it looks like to be fully alive at 51, one year older than your father ever was.
