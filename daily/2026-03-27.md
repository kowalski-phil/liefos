## What Happened

The day started with the KNIME fight still weighing on me. But something shifted over the course of the day. Yesterday's realization — "if one door closes, another opens" — stopped being a nice phrase and started feeling true. Two things made that happen.

**The slides for my boss.** With Claude Code's help, I structured a strategic slide deck and sent it to my boss essentially overnight. The quality was undeniable. He might suspect some AI assistance, but he has no idea how much leverage this gives me. Being able to produce C-level quality strategy slides in hours instead of days — that's a genuine superpower. And it positions me clearly: I'm a strategic thinker, not just an executor.

**Leading the business unit alignment.** In the afternoon, I joined a call with the business unit users — the people actually using the KNIME Business Apps, not IT. I presented my slides, and their reaction was immediate: "That's exactly what we need. Can we reuse this?" Of course they can. What matters more is what happened between the lines: I was leading that group. They followed my approach, adopted my framing, aligned behind my strategy. No org chart says I'm in charge of that circle — but functionally, I am. The thought leader. The strategic leader.

**The price of visibility.** One honest realization: this will probably create some new enemies. The primary ones right now are the people who made the phase-out decision in the first place. Under my leadership, the business unit group is opposing their proposal. Yes, we're being constructive — suggesting an improvement, not a flat rejection — but at the end of the day, they made a decision and we're challenging it. They'll feel that as opposition regardless of how diplomatically it's framed. Those are the stronger enemies right now.

Within the business unit circle itself, I should also stay aware: just because people follow my lead doesn't automatically mean I'm on friendly terms with everyone. Some may have wanted to lead this themselves, some may just not like someone stepping up. Being a little careful there is smart. But the bottom line stands: you cannot become more visible without pissing some people off. That's the cost of stepping up, and I'm willing to pay it.
