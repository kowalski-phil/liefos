## Reflection: AI, Language, and Self-Awareness

My wife had a training at her company about the dangers of AI — deepfakes, faked videos, voice cloning. They showed how just an image and a few seconds of voice are enough to fake a board member and trick people into clicking a link. Standard corporate awareness training, and I get why companies do it.

But it sparked an interesting conversation between us. I confessed that I have emotional moments with AI too — like today, reading the journal entry back and getting tears in my eyes. And I think I know why this technology hits so differently than anything before it: AI is first and foremost a language model. And language is at the core of being human. It's connected to our soul. That's why this isn't just another tech wave — it's disruptive in a way that touches something deeper.

Look at what happened here with the frustration pattern. Three weeks of journaling — just talking into a voice app and letting AI structure it — and suddenly I can see a weakness I've carried for years. The self-awareness that comes from this simple habit is genuinely helping me grow. Not toward being perfect, but toward being better for my family, for myself. If technology helps me become self-aware of my own patterns through something as simple as journaling, I honestly don't care what it costs. The result speaks for itself.

What bothers me a little is the public perception, especially in Germany. People lean hesitant. I feel for them — they're missing out. The egoistic part of me could say: good, fewer people using it means it stays my secret weapon longer. But the honest part of me thinks the greater community deserves to see both sides. Yes, there are dangers — absolutely. But don't focus only on the negative. Being aware of the dangers is actually the best baseline for leveraging the opportunities.
