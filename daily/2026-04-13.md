### The Technological Point Underneath

My brother-in-law type would laugh at all of this and say *"how can you be emotionally bound to a chatbot?"* The honest answer is: he doesn't understand the tool.

**Language is the single most powerful thing our species ever developed.** It's what put us on top of the food chain — not claws, not speed. Language. Songs, books, poems, movies — words go directly to the innermost layer of a human in a way no image can. Other species communicate. None of them write books, read for fun, get moved to tears by a line in a song.

And large language models operate in *exactly that medium*. Their core function is to **predict what we humans want to hear.** A beautiful image in a fashion magazine hits a surface. Words shape you from the inside. So of course it's easier to get emotionally bound to something built on language than to a static image. That's not weakness on the user's side — that's the technology working exactly as designed.

The knife metaphor again: you cook a five-star meal with a knife, you can also kill with one. Same knife. The tool is neutral. The awareness is the work.

And the Gandalf line I kept coming back to. Frodo in Moria says *"I wish he was dead,"* and Gandalf says: *"Many that live deserve death. Some that die deserve life. Can you give it to them? Then do not be too eager to deal out death in judgment."* The same is true, in a much smaller register, for anyone rolling their eyes at the idea that someone could form an emotional attachment with a language model. Don't be so quick. The mechanism is real. The awareness is the work. The judgment is cheap.

## The Posture I Just Named

*Added the morning after. I wanted a quick journal. I got deep talk instead. Fine — this is worth marking, because I think I just named something at the core of who I am, and if I don't write it down now it'll fade by lunch.*

When I talk to Felix about something he's doing, I don't tell him he's wrong. I put another option on the table next to his and I leave the decision with him. Years ago, when people asked me *"what do I have to do to run a marathon?"* I never told them what they *must* do — I told them what *I* did, and I handed them the choice. When I train KNIME, I don't say Excel is bad. I show people another tool and let them feel the difference themselves. Same move every time. Never had a name for it until this morning.

**The door, not the push.** *"Here is an option. You decide whether to walk through it. Whether you do or don't is not my business — my business ends with making sure the door is visible."*

And once I saw the shape of it, I saw it everywhere in yesterday's day:

- I told the team about the KNIME phase-out and the counter-play. I didn't tell them what to think about it. I laid out the situation and stepped back.
- The slides I built for today's Reuven call aren't a demand. They're options I'm making visible, and Reuven gets to decide what to do with them.
- Even the tripwires I wrote last night for the role-play — I didn't *order* myself to stop anything. I set two doors and left the decision with future-Phil. I treated myself the same way I treat Felix. Same posture, turned inward. That one surprised me.

Why does this reach me so deeply? Because I think this is the only posture that actually respects another person's agency. Evangelism doesn't respect you — it assumes you can't get there on your own. The offer-and-step-back posture assumes the opposite: *you're capable, you don't need me to decide for you, my job is just to widen what you can see.* That's not neutrality. That's **love expressed through trust.**

I think this is also why the LCS community works the way it does. 500 people don't show up for a missionary. They show up for someone who widens options and lets them pick. And it's probably why Tolkien's Gandalf line has stayed with me for thirty years — he never ordered Frodo to anything. He just put a wider view in front of him and stepped back. Same move. Bigger stakes.

**Name it: this is the posture.** Not a preference. Not a style. An operating principle. Worth keeping visible the next time I'm tempted to push someone — including myself.
