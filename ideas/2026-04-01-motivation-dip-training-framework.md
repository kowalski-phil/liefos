# The Motivation Dip — Training Framework

## Core Idea

Build a "motivation dip" module into all training offerings (KNIME, AI, prompting). Name the dip before it happens so learners recognize it as progress, not failure.

## The Framework

Every new skill follows the same curve:
1. **Novelty high** — excitement, curiosity, "this is amazing"
2. **The dip** — motivation drops, things don't work, "maybe this isn't for me"
3. **Habit formation** — push through, it becomes routine, real competence builds

**Key teaching move:** Tell students up front: *"At some point in this course, you'll want to quit. Here's why that's the best sign you're on the right track."*

## Phil's Proof Points (with dates)

- **Journaling:** 10+ physical journals failed over the years. Voice-first approach (March 2026) → survived the dip → 31 consecutive days. The medium was the barrier, not the motivation.
- **Ironman training:** 1.5 years including motivation valleys → pushed through → Challenge Roth 2025, 13:56:23
- **KNIME career:** Started in procurement/finance → learned a platform nobody asked him to learn → 25 years later, leading the service he helped create
- **AI adoption:** From "the KNIME guy" to building AI consulting practice, Claude Code projects — all because he didn't stop at "this is confusing"

Four domains, same curve, every time. Not anecdotal — a pattern with receipts.

## Where to Use

- **Level 2 Siemens training** (next 1-2 weeks) — include as an early slide, not buried at the end
- **After-training surveys** — ask: "Did you experience the motivation dip we talked about? What helped you push through?"
- **All future training offerings** — prompting workshops, Claude Code training, enterprise cohorts
- **Newsletter / YouTube** — this is a story-first topic that fits the ARM method perfectly

## Why This Matters

Shows trainer credibility ("I've been there, I went through this"). Reframes the dip from a reason to quit into a predictable milestone. Gives learners permission to struggle without interpreting it as personal failure.
