## The Moment You Want to Quit Is the Moment It's Working

I've tried journaling at least ten times.

Physical journals. Beautiful ones. Expensive ones. Even took an online course about it. Never made it past eleven days. Not once.

### Attempt Number Eleven

A month ago, I tried again. Different approach this time — voice instead of writing. I speak my thoughts, AI transcribes and organizes them. That's it.

The first week was electric. I couldn't wait to do it. New tool, new process, everything felt fresh.

Then week two hit.

The novelty wore off. Entries got shorter. That little voice showed up: "Do I really need to do this today?"

Sound familiar?

Here's the thing — I've seen this exact pattern before. Not just with journaling.

When I started training for my Ironman, the first few weeks were pure adrenaline. Then came the months where every training session felt like a chore. I pushed through. Crossed the finish line at Challenge Roth in 13:56:23.

When I first picked up KNIME over a decade ago, it was exciting. Then it got confusing. I kept going. Today I lead a community of 500+ people using it daily.

Same curve. Every time.

### The Part Nobody Warns You About

There's a dip between "this is exciting" and "this is a habit." It's the place where most people quit — not because the thing isn't working, but because the novelty ran out and discipline hasn't kicked in yet.

I'm now on day 31 of journaling. It's not exciting anymore. It's better than that. It's routine.

And that experience got me thinking about how we train people.

Whether I'm teaching KNIME, AI tools, or automation workflows — my participants will hit this dip. Guaranteed. And if nobody tells them it's coming, they'll interpret it as "this isn't for me."

So I've started naming it upfront: *"At some point during this training, something won't work. You'll feel frustrated. You'll wonder if this was worth your time. That's not failure. That's the part right before it clicks."*

It doesn't remove the dip. But it changes what it means.

### What's Your Dip?

Think about a skill you tried to learn and gave up on. Was it really not for you — or did you just stop at the hard part?

Phil "Day 31" Kowalski
