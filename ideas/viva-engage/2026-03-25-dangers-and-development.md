## My Wife Learned About AI Dangers. I Used AI to Work on a Weakness.

### The Trigger

My wife came home from a training recently. Part of it covered AI dangers — deepfakes, voice cloning, how a single image and a few seconds of audio can fake a board member convincingly enough to trick people.

Fair enough. Important stuff. I'm glad companies do this.

But it triggered a conversation between us that went deeper. Because when I look at the broader picture — media coverage, political debates, community discussions — the dominant narrative around AI in Germany seems to be: regulate first. Be careful. This is dangerous.

And I get the instinct. But it's incomplete.

### The Other Side

For three weeks now, I've been journaling daily. I speak my thoughts into a voice app, and AI structures them into a journal entry. Simple habit. Ten minutes.

Here's what changed: I've known for years that I have a frustration pattern — small setbacks triggering disproportionate reactions. That's not news to me. What's new is that consistent journaling with AI makes me **more aware** of it. I see it written down. I see it repeated across entries. And that awareness — not the AI itself, but the awareness it creates — helps me actually work on it.

If a simple habit like this, supported by technology, helps me improve as a person — why would I focus only on what could go wrong?

### The Real Gap

Nobody is saying the dangers aren't real. They are. Deepfakes are real. Voice cloning is real. Awareness of that is a necessary baseline.

But a baseline is where you **start**. Not where you stop.

What I observe in Germany — in media, in politics, sometimes even in our own conversations at work — is that the discussion often ends at "be careful." Careful becomes hesitant. Hesitant becomes "not for me."

Meanwhile, the technology is helping people do things they couldn't do before. Not just faster spreadsheets or automated emails. Real, personal development. Through something as basic as structured reflection.

AI is a language model. Language is how we think. That's why this technology feels different from anything before it.

**Are we spending too much time on what could go wrong — and too little on what's already going right?**

Phil "Dangers and Development" Kowalski

*This is my personal opinion and experience, not an official Siemens position.*
