## Three Countries. One HIIT Session. No Office.

Yesterday at the end of the work day, I was gasping for air at my own desk.

30 minutes of HIIT. Heart rate somewhere it hadn't been in a while. I couldn't even do all the exercises — modified a couple, skipped one completely. Classic first session after a drift week.

But the part I actually want to tell you about isn't the sweat.

### Four People, Three Countries, One Teams Call

The person running the session was in Munich. A colleague was in Portugal. Another one somewhere else in Germany. I was here near Nuremberg. We trained *together*. Same time. Same exercises. Same panting. Four tiles on one Microsoft Teams call, everyone's living room / office / kitchen / wherever serving as a makeshift gym.

My boss Ruben put us on a bi-weekly plan to prepare for **B2Run** — the Munich company run in July. Ruben takes it seriously in the best way: ambitious, but the point is the fun. Yesterday was our very first session. In two weeks it's my turn to host, and with a few Ironman reps still in the legs I'm already sketching out a runner-friendly mix for the group.

### The Script That Quietly Died

Here's the thing that stuck with me after I finally caught my breath.

I had a script running in my head I didn't even know was there:

*"Physical training together only works if you're in the same room."*

It felt obvious. Self-evident. The kind of assumption you don't examine because why would you? Training is physical. Team training is social. Same room, same sweat. That's the deal, right?

Wrong.

Once you actually *see* it working — once you're watching a colleague in Portugal push through burpees on your screen while your own legs are shaking — the script just quietly dies. You can't unsee it. It turns out the "default" was never a default at all. It was a habit of thought from a world where the tools didn't exist yet.

### The Bigger Question

B2Run HIIT training is a small example. But I keep asking myself: **how many other "has to be in person" things on my calendar are just untested defaults?**

Team offsites. Brainstorms. Celebrations. Onboarding. Coffee chats with new colleagues. The rulebook we're all still running was mostly written before Teams, before high-quality video, before we'd spent years figuring out how distributed work actually feels.

The tools have caught up. A lot of our rules haven't.

### One Thing to Try This Week

Pick one thing on your calendar this week that "obviously has to be in person." Ask yourself honestly: *has anyone on the team ever tried it another way?* If the answer is no, maybe it's worth testing once.

Worst case, you learn why the old way was right and you go back to it with better reasons.

Best case, a script in your head quietly dies — and a little more of your work life gets a lot more flexible.

Phil "Sweating at My Desk" Kowalski
