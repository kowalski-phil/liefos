## Key Takeaways

### Why education wins as a business model
- Teaching people to build absolves you of taking requirements and jumping through hoops that stall progress
- Content you create will "rhyme" across clients — not repeated, but leverageable
- You become the trusted expert, then pick what projects to take on
- Recurring revenue: the space changes so fast that education never goes stale
- "Teach to fish" model works as a sales approach — flips the dynamic vs. pitching custom dev

### The Trojan Horse model
1. Education gets you in the door
2. You become the trusted internal guru
3. This opens doors to audits, custom dev, retainers — by choice
4. Community/retainer becomes scalable recurring revenue

### Who's in the room (discovery framework)
- **Skeptics** — anti-AI, often worried about jobs. Validate their real concerns first, then pivot with "baby with the bathwater" argument
- **Champions** — power users, sometimes undercover. Amazing for getting you brought back
- **Excited newbies** — most common. Need a quick win fast or they become skeptics
- **Overwhelmed** — paralyzed by too many tools/options. Need clear direction

### Discovery questions for teaching engagements
1. Where are most people at? (101 vs 301 split)
2. What would excite them most? (the "golden nugget" demo)
3. Who are the skeptics and why?
4. What are the most annoying tasks? (pain = your demo)
5. Who's the internal champion?
6. What's their urgency?

### Claude Code vs Claude Co-Work split
- Most cohorts: 70% Co-Work, 30% Code
- Code: technical users, curious PMs, needs integrations/automations
- Co-Work: non-technical, large orgs with IT bottlenecks, thinking/writing/strategy

### Student journey
1. Awareness — tried Claude once, unconvinced
2. Aha moment — first time Claude does something they genuinely couldn't
3. Capability — prompting well, building workflows faster
4. Independence — still need someone to drop the latest knowledge
