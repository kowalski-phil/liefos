# Week 4 — "The most dangerous report is the one nobody reads"

**Subject:** She deleted half her workbook.

---

Ever automated something that shouldn't exist in the first place?

Remember Tina? Treasury. Three bank CSVs. Automated cash position report.

Last week, she caught her workflow silently dropping rows. Fixed it with a Row Splitter.

But that review file — the one catching the weird rows — made her look at the report differently.

Not at the data. At the *structure*.

Twelve sections. Twelve tabs. Built up over years by three different people who had the job before her.

She'd automated all of them. Proudly.

But that dangerous question wouldn't leave her alone:

"What else in this report goes unnoticed?"

So she did something brave. She walked over to her manager — the one who actually received the report — and asked:

"Which sections do you use?"

The answer nearly knocked her off her chair.

Three.

Out of twelve.

Three sections. That's what he actually looked at. The rest? "I think Finance used to need those."

*Used to.*

Tina had spent weeks automating nine sections that nobody read.

The fix wasn't a node. It wasn't a workflow. It wasn't even KNIME.

It was a conversation.

One question. Five minutes. And then Tina did something most people can't bring themselves to do.

She sat down with her manager, went through the workbook tab by tab, and crossed off everything he didn't need.

Nine tabs. Gone. The formulas behind them. Gone. The three KNIME nodes feeding them. Gone.

What was left? A clean, 3-tab report her manager actually opened on purpose. Not out of obligation. Out of interest.

No tool can fix a report that shouldn't exist.

Ask the person who receives your report: "Which section do you actually use?" You might kill half the workbook in one conversation.

Tina's report is fixed. But every morning, someone still has to manually email it, format it, paste it into another system. Next week: what happens when your tools don't talk to each other.

Phil "Delete Half The Workbook" Kowalski

PS: I've started asking one question before I accept any automation project: *"Why do we need this report?"*

If the answer is "because we've always done it" — that's not a reason. That's a habit.
