Subject: We checked the checks of the checks

---

Ever stayed past midnight at the office — not because of a crisis, but because of a spreadsheet?

Fiscal year-end closing. The big one.

Every year, same ritual. Same dread.

The regular month-end was bad enough. Quarter-end was worse. But fiscal year-end? That was the boss fight.

Late-night shifts. Fluorescent lights humming. Cold coffee. The cleaning crew giving you sympathetic looks on their way out.

And the spreadsheets. Oh, the spreadsheets.

We didn't just have reports. We had control tables.

Control tables that checked the reports. Control tables that checked the control tables. And — I swear I'm not making this up — control tables that checked the control tables that checked the control tables.

Three layers deep. Just to make sure the numbers were right.

Because they HAD to be right. The CFO would go through every line. Every. Single. Line.

One wrong number and you'd hear about it. Not in a nice way.

So we checked. And double-checked. And triple-checked.

Nobody had weekends during closing season. That wasn't a complaint — it was just how things were.

We accepted it. Like weather.

But here's what kept nagging me: we weren't doing WORK. We were checking work. Checking the checks. Checking the checks of the checks.

That's not accounting. That's a hamster wheel with a suit on.

Here's the thing — nobody questioned it. Not for years.

For eight years, I spent closing season doing the same thing.

"That's how we've always done it." The most dangerous sentence in any finance department.

88% of spreadsheets contain errors. I believe it. I've personally hunted down enough of them.

So we built layers of control to catch what the tool couldn't prevent.

But no amount of checking fixes a broken foundation.

A spreadsheet with three layers of control tables isn't a report. It's a confession that the tool isn't up to the job.

And I didn't see that for a long time. None of us did.

If your report needs a control table — and your control table needs its own control table — stop checking. Start questioning the tool.

Next week, I'll tell you about someone who had three source files feeding one of these monster spreadsheets.

They killed it with two nodes. The whole thing. Gone.

Phil "Control Table of Control Tables" Kowalski
